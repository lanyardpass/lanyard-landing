## The honest state of play

Let's get the facts straight before any opinions, because this is the part that actually changes your decision, and it's the part that keeps moving.

As of June 2026, none of the four current Universal Orlando tiers, Seasonal, Power, Preferred, or Premier, include admission to Epic Universe. Every tier needs a separately purchased Epic ticket. The good news, and the part the official pages bury, is that every tier is eligible for the discounted passholder Epic ticket. There's no tier restriction on the discount itself.

Two more things worth knowing so the picture is complete. First, the lineup right now is 2-Park only: the 3-Park passes that added Volcano Bay were suspended in late 2025 ahead of Volcano Bay's long refurbishment closure, so you can't buy one today anyway. Second, some of your pass perks *do* carry over to Epic once you've paid for admission.

So it's a lopsided deal, and I'd rather be straight with you about it. Once you've actually paid your way into Epic, your pass does some real work, the free or discounted parking and the dining and merch discounts are genuinely nice if you hold Preferred or Premier. Where it does nothing for you is getting in the door or getting ahead of a line. There's no early entry at Epic, and no Express for any tier, not even Premier (Universal floated that in April 2025, then walked it back). There's no passholder lounge inside the park either. And you can't just bolt Epic onto your pass. However you slice it, the only way in is that separate, discounted ticket.

<Callout variant="info" title="What an Epic day ticket runs a passholder" icon="🎟️">
Roughly **$94 to $179** depending on the date, about 10 to 15% off public pricing. It's dynamic, so off-peak dates are dramatically cheaper. Late-August 2026 dates were seen as low as **$94 before tax**, the lowest passholder pricing yet, while peak holiday dates push toward $179. The date you pick matters more than your tier.
</Callout>

One thing to keep an eye on. Those Epic perks are locked in through **December 31, 2026**. Universal already extended them once, which is encouraging, but it's still a hard end date, and they could just as easily lapse after that. So count on them through 2026, and check the date at the top before you bank on anything past it.

## What Epic is actually like

Universal did not hold back on this park. Stardust Racers is a racing coaster that bends and twists in ways I'd never felt before. Monsters Unchained packs in more animatronics and theming than any Universal ride I can name short of Battle at the Ministry, and it shows. Even Hiccup's Wing Gliders, a family coaster, is a fully immersive ride. But all that ambition has a price. The more complex the ride, the more there is to break, and uptime can be a real issue at Epic. So build some slack into your day and don't pin your whole plan on any one headliner being open.

As an Orlando local and a passholder, I've been fortunate enough to have been to Epic Universe 4 times. Once during passholder preview, before it officially opened, once shortly after it opened, and then twice in 2026, including my most recent trip in March. Those last two trips were also with friends, so I got to see Epic Universe through fresh eyes and shared experiences.

The experience you have at Epic Universe is going to be wholly dependent on how crowded the park is the day you visit. If you're a Florida local, you have a bit of control over when you decide to head to the park, if you're visiting, you're going to be generalizing crowds based on seasonality, not watching for an oddly "light" period of time. In either case, the experience is truly a tale of two very different Epic Universes.

First, let's explore the reasons that crowd size changes the experience, because that fact feels self evident, but you also need to understand how different Epic Universe is from something like Magic Kingdom, a park that can handle massive crowds and still feel like there are options and things you can explore without standing in line being your majority pastime.

Magic Kingdom has over 40 rides and attractions, Epic Universe has 13. This fact alone is going to concentrate the crowds significantly. To add to that fact, a large number of these rides are functionally difficult to operate when it is raining. In fact, only 3 rides and 2 shows are indoors at Epic. That ratio is actually in contrast to Magic Kingdom, which has about half of the rides and attractions indoors, and when you're comparing 20 things to do in a common Florida summer thunderstorm vs 5 things to do, you're going to feel that pain quickly.

So all of this lends to the potential for VERY long wait times at Epic Universe. Picture the same park on two different days. On a packed day, those 13 attractions feel like 5. You spend most of your day in line, watching a 100-minute wait crawl down for Mine-Cart, and the second an afternoon storm rolls in, you and everyone else are fighting over the same handful of indoor spots. You ride four, maybe five things, and you walk out not only wondering what all the fuss was about... but wondering why you paid for that experience in the first place.

Now picture a light day. You walk onto Stardust Racers, then turn around and ride it again. You're actually wandering Celestial Park instead of shuffling through a switchback. You've got time to sit down for a real meal, and you stay for the night show because, why not, you've already done everything you came for. Same park, same 13 attractions, completely different day. That's the one you remember.

## Is an Epic day worth it, and when to go

So when is it worth adding a visit to Epic Universe if you already hold a UOAP? Honestly, whenever you want to! Keep in mind, Universal isn't going to just gift us Epic Universe onto our current pass for no cost. This park is brand new, and they already have capacity issues, I would imagine the upgrade to include Epic Universe is going to easily be 50% of the current cost of our passes, and even then it may still be gated behind either a reservation system, or a certain number of opportunities to visit per year, or both. But if you want to go, go.

When to go is its own question, and here's my honest take. I'm a data person, so it pains me to say this, but the data just isn't there yet. Epic didn't exist eighteen months ago, and there aren't enough seasons of crowd numbers to reliably call the single "best" day to visit. You'll see plenty of "go on a Tuesday in September" takes, and beyond basic seasonality, I think that's mostly a guess, especially because Universal keeps changing the ticketing under everyone's feet. It used to be one-day tickets only; now there are multi-day tickets, day packages, and bundles that didn't exist six months ago, and every change scrambles the patterns again. So I don't chase a magic date. I just watch the dynamic pricing calendar, because the price doubles as a crowd signal, since Universal charges more on the days it expects to be busy. The one catch is the weekend. Universal charges more for weekends no matter what, so a pricey Saturday can actually be less crowded than a cheaper-looking weekday. The fix is to compare like with like, weekdays against weekdays and weekends against other weekends. Do that, follow the pricing to the cheaper day, and you'll usually land on the quieter one.

<Callout variant="note" title="What Epic days cost, spread out vs. all at once" icon="🧮">
Since Epic isn't on your pass, every visit is a separate ticket, and how you space them changes the math entirely.

Spread across the year, the way a passholder normally goes, you pay per visit, around $140 a day:

- **2 visits**, about **$280**
- **4 visits**, about **$560**
- **6 visits**, about **$840**
- **12 visits**, about **$1,680**

There's no cheap way to pre-buy scattered days like that. But if you'd rather go hard on Epic in one stretch, the math flips. Universal's multi-day promos (a recent "3 days plus 2 free" is five single-park days inside a 7-day window) don't restrict which park you pick each day. And as a passholder you already have Universal Studios and Islands of Adventure, so there's no reason to spend any of those five days there. Put all five at Epic, and a multi-park ticket turns into a backdoor five-day Epic ticket, as low as **~$310**, around **$62 a day**, less than half the per-visit rate above. The trade-off is it's a binge, not a year-long pace, five days in one week, not one a month. Promos like this come and go, so check what's running, but when one is, it's the cheapest Epic you'll find.
</Callout>

## How to do Epic right

There is good news, while you won't always know which Epic you're walking into, but you can stack the deck toward the second one, the light day you remember. Part of that is setting your expectations going in, part of it is a handful of habits once you're there. Here's how I do it.

### Before you go

**Skip the Express Pass.** Is Express Pass worth it at Epic? For most passholders, no, and it comes down to the price. It's just outrageously overpriced for what you get. The only real exception is if Epic is a true one-and-done for you, one day and that's it. Otherwise, do the math. On a high-demand date, Express can run $360 a person. For that same money you could come to Epic two or three separate times, and still ride everything you want, just spread across days. You get so much more in the bargain, too. A different restaurant each trip, character meet-and-greets, and the freedom to bail on a washout afternoon instead of gambling your one expensive day on the weather. Forcing all of Epic into a single day to justify a $300-plus Express Pass is the opposite of how a passholder should think. Epic isn't on your pass, but the mindset still wins. Get your value over a few visits instead of cramming one frantic, pricey day.

**Get a Power-Up Band, especially if Nintendo raised you.** I went to Epic three times without a Power-Up Band, splurged on one the fourth trip, and my only regret was not grabbing it the first time. Without a band, Super Nintendo World can actually be a little overwhelming, a wall of color and noise washing over you with nothing to do but look at it. The band flips that completely. Suddenly you're interacting with the land and the app, hunting and exploring for hidden stuff, and you can happily lose hours to it. Is it absolutely necessary? No. But if you grew up playing Nintendo, and as a kid of the eighties I sure did, it's one of those small things that genuinely elevates the whole Epic day. Get it on your first visit, not your fourth.

**Pack a fanny pack, and mind what's in it.** A small fanny pack or cross-body bag is all I bring, every single day, and it earns its keep two ways. First, the lockers. A few rides make you stow your stuff, but a small waist or shoulder bag will get you onto almost anything that doesn't have a metal detector. I've kept mine on for Monsters Unchained, Hiccup's Wing Gliders, Battle at the Ministry, you name it. The one ride where it won't fly is Stardust Racers, whose metal detector sends everything to a locker. Just keep the bag small; full over-the-shoulder backpacks won't get the same pass. Second, what's in it. A small collapsible silicone water cup is the MVP. Every Coke Freestyle machine gives out free water, so you walk up, fill it, and skip the six-dollar Dasani. That works at every Universal property, not just Epic, and it pays for the cup in an afternoon. Round it out with an extra phone battery, sunglasses, and sunscreen. Sunscreen especially, because Epic is a brand-new park, the foliage hasn't grown in, so you're in the open far more than at Islands of Adventure or Universal Studios. Here, sun protection isn't optional.

### Working the day

**Rope drop.** For the uninitiated, rope drop is arriving at the park and being ready to ~~run~~ briskly walk in as the park opens. This is not as simple as showing up 10 min early, the rope drop experience itself is a line. But, and this is key, if you're maximizing for as much as possible in a single day, that is an unpaid line. If that sounds unintuitive, let me explain. I often hear people say... "Why are you going to the park early, just to stand in line." This sounds reasonable on its face, but really comes down to maximizing your paid experience. Meaning, especially when Epic itself isn't included in your Annual Pass, you are essentially experiencing the park the same way anyone does, that is, a one time cost that is reflected in a finite amount of time spent in the park. Showing up before the park opens to get in line, you're trading some additional free time for more paid experience. So if maximizing what you get to experience in your given day at Epic is your goal, rope drop is a must. Get there early (as much as 60 min before park opening), and head for the attraction that interests you the most, assuming that attraction is open. Now that only works if you've maximized your rope drop potential and you're near the front of the line the time the park opens, if you missed that window, your strategy shifts, which is, go to the part of the park fewer people are heading, because you're not going to beat them to the headline attractions, so now your goal is to try and sneak in a few rides on attractions that aren't the main draw. As an example, if you arrive closer to opening, and you see the crowd heading right to Harry Potter and the Battle at the Ministry, I would instead head to Isle of Berk, and get in rides on Hiccup's Wing Gliders and Dragon Racer's Rally.

If everyone is running to Super Nintendo World to ride Mine-Cart Madness or Mario Kart: Bowser's Challenge, instead run back to ride Curse of the Werewolf or Stardust Racers. It can be advantageous to run and grab a few lower wait experiences in the morning, letting the large crowd run into headliners. Also remember, those staying at a Universal hotel have a head start (Early Park Admission, an hour earlier than those not staying at a Universal hotel, and a hotel perk, not a pass perk), and those staying at Helios Grand have both a head start and an exclusive separate entrance from you. And if Epic is your only day, treat it like someone without an annual pass would. Get there early, make a whole day of it, and save your "run in, ride Velocicoaster once, and leave" mentality for the legacy parks.

**Use single-rider lines, but don't skip the showpiece queues.** If you're riding solo, absolutely use them. They're free and they save real time. They do skip most of the queue, though, and at Epic the queues are part of the show. They're themed so well, Monsters Unchained, Mario Kart, and especially Battle at the Ministry, that blowing past them your first time means missing a chunk of the experience you came for. So my rule is simple. Single-rider once you've already walked the full queue, or on rides where the line isn't much to look at. Mine-Cart Madness is the easy example, the queue's kind of boring, so single-ride that one guilt-free. But Battle at the Ministry? There's a single-rider line, and no, don't use it your first time. That queue is genuinely breathtaking, one of the best at Epic, and worth every minute.

**Keep an eye on the weather.** Florida weather is notorious for summer thunderstorms. They happen quite literally, almost every single day in the summer. If I'm at the park and it looks like it is calling for afternoon thunderstorms, I may grab a light snack around lunch time, if the weather is holding, instead of a full meal, knowing that gives me the option of jumping into one of the many amazing sit down & quick service restaurants at Epic around the 2p-3p time when those afternoon thunderstorms hit, which gives me both reprieve from the heat, and I get to avoid the lunch rush.

### Eat well

Since I brought up food, let's talk about it. I mentioned earlier that Epic Universe only has 13 attractions... that is true if you aren't a foodie. If you like to eat, Epic Universe has a LOT more attractions. Having myself grown up in the NE US, Cedar Point was my go to park experience, and I must say, Epic Universe is worlds different from your typical amusement park fare. In fact, it was reported that Universal had to add chicken fingers and fries to a few of the restaurants shortly after opening because many parents had their more finicky eating children losing their minds that they just wanted their typical go to. None of the food at Epic Universe is typical, it's all well themed, very delicious, and priced accordingly. So come hungry, lean in, and try something you normally wouldn't get, I think you'll be pleasantly surprised.

I've eaten at both full-service spots, Atlantic and Blue Dragon, and neither is a skip. Don't try to do both in one day, though, unless you're starving or you're splitting them across lunch and dinner. Atlantic is the special one. It's beautiful, and if you can land a table down by the fountain, you're sitting almost level with the water. Blue Dragon is genuinely delicious and themed beautifully for an Asian restaurant. But my actual favorite is the hidden gem, The Oak & Star Tavern, the barbecue spot in Celestial Park. I've eaten there three of my four visits. It's fast-casual, you order from your phone, and for whatever reason it's never slammed the way the rest of the park is. The food's genuinely good, the portions are decent, and it's the best barbecue I've had in any theme park. If I had to pick one must-try, it isn't some viral snack, it's the mango bread here. Get the mango bread.

The one I haven't managed is Toadstool Cafe in Super Nintendo World, widely called the best-themed restaurant in the park. It doesn't take reservations, which is exactly why there's always a line. So you time it instead. Go before the lunch rush, or swing by mid-afternoon once the lunch crowd has cleared. Super Nintendo World is the most crowded land in the park, so with Toadstool, timing is the whole game.

And the famous one, the mac and cheese cones at Hooligan's Grog & Gruel. I'll say it, they're a little overrated. They're fun, and who doesn't love an edible cone, but they're not worth standing in a 45-minute line for. Think of it like a giant pretzel or a corn dog at a regular amusement park, except this is Epic's upscale, carb-heavy version. Grab one if the line's short, or mobile-order it at Spit Fyre Grill to skip the wait, enjoy it for what it is, and don't build your day around it.

### Don't miss

**The single biggest mistake is skipping the shows.** I haven't said much about the shows, so let me be direct. Do not skip them. Le Cirque Arcanus and The Untrainable Dragon are the most impressive theatrical experiences I've seen at any theme park, full stop. This isn't the usual dancing-and-singing-to-the-soundtrack filler. It's real animatronics, full set design, choreography, special effects, lighting, and interactive moments with the crowd. If you're someone who normally walks past theme-park shows, Epic is where you break that habit. Skip these and you've missed some of the best things in the park.

**A hidden gem at the Burning Blade Tavern.** Plenty of guides will tell you the Burning Blade Tavern in Dark Universe goes up in flames on a timer, and that a bell rings right before it does. True. But the fire isn't even the best part. If you stand there and get quiet, the whole thing is a little scene. Listen and you'll hear the villagers coming, a mob moving through the speakers, and the lighting sells it, the glow of implied pitchforks and torches closing in right before it ignites. Stand to the left of the tavern and you can actually hear the townsfolk running through the woods, shouting, and watch the glow of their torches move through the trees. Then it catches. It's a tiny, perfect piece of immersive theater that everyone checking it off their list walks right past.

**Stay until close.** For some reason, many people do not stay until park close. I believe it's a combination of factors leading to this, but it presents a real opportunity for both a local and someone looking to maximize their one day at Epic. Namely, the lines tend to drop after 8p. That 60 min Hiccup's ride can drop to 25 min. That 100 min wait for Mine-Cart drops to 45 min. It's not a guarantee, but it happens often enough in my experience that it's worth staying. There is one other big reason to stick around, the closing spectacular!

## Is your pass still worth it, given Epic?

Much of this raises the question, is having an annual pass still worth it without Epic Universe? And my answer is unequivocally yes, your pass is still worth it. The reason is simple, the opening of Epic Universe didn't change anything about the current passes and structure. In fact, some of the perks available to you at the other parks, still apply to you at Epic Universe. So for those passes, when you do head over to Epic, you actually gain that value for no additional cost.

That being said, not being able to enjoy Epic Universe in the same way we've grown accustomed to exploring Islands of Adventure or Universal Studios is a bummer, and if you're in the camp that refuses to renew until they add Epic Universe, I understand the urge. But let me revisit the pass value again to remind you of what you're getting.

Using the Lanyard Pass Calculator below, and choosing the Preferred Pass, which is currently the best value for the money in my opinion, plug in a Florida resident, 2-Park and you'll see what it runs. If you're renewing in full, you're getting 20% off, which means your pass pays for itself in only 3 or 4 visits depending on your current status. Let's see how that breaks down throughout the year if you can make the trip at least 4 times a year:

1. **Mardi Gras.** I don't know about you, but I'm going to make at least one trip to Universal for Mardi Gras. The food alone makes this worth it, but I also really enjoy the energy, and well, I don't ever want my feet to fail me! (1st visit of 4 needed)
2. **Holidays at Universal.** You get to experience both the Universal holiday parade with the Macy's balloons as well as Grinchmas, both fun experiences in their own right. Also, the Hogwarts holiday show is amazing... I'm going at least once. (2nd visit of 4 needed)
3. **Halloween Horror Nights.** This is a separately ticketed event, but if you're so inclined, your pass still pulls its weight: you get parking, and because you're already in the park during the day, you can get in line for the houses before the event officially starts. The folks who show up just for HHN don't get in until it opens. (3rd visit of 4)
